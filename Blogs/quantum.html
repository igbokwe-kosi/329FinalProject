Quantum Computing and the Evolution of Computational Power

By Diego de Jaraiz

The Future in Computing Technology

Over the years, computing power has advanced significantly, defying initial expectations. With the introduction of the integrated circuit or microchip, a few years later, a new law was founded, called Moore's Law. This law states that every two years, the number of transistors on an integrated circuit doubles, doubling the computer’s performance. A transistor is a tiny electronic device that acts like an on/off switch, controlling the flow of 1s and 0s in a computer to process information. Moore’s law has remained true since 1965, resulting in microchips now featuring billions of densely packed transistors.

However, the continued effort to make transistors smaller, a crucial factor in Moore's Law, is reaching physical limits. The spatial constraints between transistors have become so small that further size reduction is challenging. This development implies an impending obsolescence of Moore's Law. The critical question emerges: How will the industry keep on improving computing power when the physical space for additional transistors on a microchip is rapidly diminishing?

Introduction to Quantum Computers

The industry has been exploring diverse paths for advancing computing power, with Quantum Computers emerging as a promising recent development. Quantum computers utilize principles from quantum mechanics to perform complex calculations beyond the capabilities of traditional computers. Unlike classical computers, quantum computers employ quantum bits, or qubits, which can exist in two states simultaneously through a phenomenon known as "superposition." This unique property allows quantum computers to process and store information more extensively.

In addition to superposition, quantum computers use another quantum mechanics concept called "entanglement." This occurs when a pair of electrons or protons become interconnected, establishing a mutual dependence regardless of their spatial separation. In the realm of quantum computers, entanglement manifests as all qubits being entangled, creating interdependence among their states. As a result, these entangled qubits can communicate with each other instantaneously, overcoming any conflicts arising from spatial distances.

How will Quantum Computers Affect the Modern World?

Quantum computers, much like classical ones did in the past, will very possibly redefine computing's limits. The potential computing power they harbor could mark the pinnacle of computing power. This shift also implies that encryption algorithms may soon become obsolete. The essential questions are: How exactly will encryption methods become obsolete? And, in what ways will the computational power of quantum computers influence different encryption types?

In the near future, it's likely that quantum computers will have enough power to make current encryption methods outdated. But how? Quantum computers excel at processing large numbers rapidly, surpassing classical supercomputers. Shor's algorithm, specifically designed for quantum computers, enables efficient factorization of large numbers. While the widely used RSA encryption algorithm currently remains secure, the potential threat comes from the rapid data deciphering capabilities of quantum computers. This capability may cause RSA and similar encryption methods to become obsolete soon. Anticipating the superior computational capabilities of quantum computers, factorizing these large numbers could take mere hours with a 4,000 qubit quantum computer, a task that would require classical supercomputers millions of years to accomplish. This implies that someone with a quantum computer could potentially access your bank account within hours.

With the rise of Quantum Computers, it's clear that new algorithms will be developed to match the computational power of these systems, yet, as always, the captivating question persists: How?

The Development of New Algorithms

The National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) has unveiled four cryptographic algorithms designed to resist the potential threat posed by quantum computers. These algorithms, although still in development, have a mathematical foundation that makes it challenging for both quantum and classical computers to decrypt. While they are not finalized, these algorithms represent a significant step in creating tools that can withstand the computational power of quantum computers.

In addition to NIST's algorithm development, there is also an act called the Endless Frontiers Act that includes 100 billion dollars in federal funding to support quantum and post-quantum encryptions. This funding aims to prepare our modern world for the potential impact of quantum computers in the future.

Quantum computers are bound to reshape the modern digital landscape, prompting significant changes in cybersecurity. The key question is: When will quantum computers reach the required level of power to be a threat?

When Will Quantum Computers Become a Thing?

Current quantum computers lack the computational power to be a threat to our digital security. Presently, these computers don't possess enough qubits to be sufficiently powerful. According to Rose's law, similar to Moore's law, the quantum computer's qubit count doubles every 2 years. IBM's quantum computer Osprey currently leads with 433 qubits. However, a challenge arises for all quantum computers as some qubits are utilized for error correction, reducing the number of qubits available for calculations. Breaking RSA encryption in hours would require 4,000 qubits. If Rose's law holds, as Moore's law did for over 50 years, we can anticipate quantum computers surpassing 4,000 qubits in 6 to 8 years, leading to a redefined algorithmic landscape.

Apart from Threats: What are the Positive Changes Coming from Quantum Computers?

Quantum computers were not designed to break modern encryptions but to revolutionize the way we see computers in our present day. Quantum computers will transform the medical industry by simulating molecular-level processes with exceptional accuracy. These simulations offer a faster and more precise environment for future medical studies and testing compared to real-world scenarios. Beyond medicine, quantum computers' precise simulations can benefit industries like electric cars. The accurate simulations made possible by quantum computers could lead to the creation of safer, easier-to-recycle, and more energy-dense batteries for electric cars using new materials.

The computational power of a quantum computer doesn't just help us create detailed simulations; it also allows us to build more advanced machine-learning programs. Quantum computers process data faster, enhancing their ability for better pattern recognition, predictions, and decision-making algorithms.

The arrival of Quantum Computers marks a significant shift, not just in the digital landscape but in our everyday reality. Quantum computers are here to stay, and the influence they'll wield in the future will be even more impactful than we might currently realize.
