Is Public Wi-Fi Safe?!

By Seth Pullishy

When we are out and about, we frequently need Wi-Fi for googling things like restaurant hours, Google Maps, responding to family members, and far more. Traditionally we would need to use data to access this information, but more and more stores are now getting public Wi-Fi with some requiring just an email, and some an account to connect to it. Despite the incredible convenience, are these safe to use? If not, what is the risk and what can we and society do to protect each other?

But before we talk about those, we need to talk about what makes a network unsecure. The first is lack of encryption (explained in the next paragraph), weak network password (makes it easier for an attacker to sign in), and outdated software (importance explained on a different page). This makes it unsecure because it makes it easier for a bad actor to get in and get information. We’re gonna talk about some risks, sometimes how to fix the

First, though, how can public Wi-Fi be risky? Well, if it is “unsecured”, or in other words if it has some security flaws. Some very common ones include lack of encryption (data going in and out isn’t scrambled), weak network passwords, and outdated software (bad guys tech may be better than the companies). Any of these issues can make a network much more susceptible to the bad guys, keeping these in mind we would like to talk about how a bad guy can get to you through a public Wi-Fi network if it is unsecured.

The first risk is eavesdropping, many of the public Wi-Fi networks are not encrypted. This simply means that your data is changed to be unreadable to bad actors, and then decrypted once it is done being moved around. Because many of these networks are not encrypted those bad actors could read sensitive information like login credentials, personal messages and more. The solution could involve simply not signing in (boring), or more interestingly using a VPN, covered on a different page of our website!

Another risk is fake networks, let’s say that you’re going to Walmart and see two different networks: “Walmart Guest” and “WALMART-GUEST”. Flip a coin and sign into one. If you choose wrong, a cybercriminal now can intercept your data. This is called a man-in-the-middle attack. If you would like to avoid this, try and connect to networks who require a password given at the front desk or once again use a VPN.

Weak network password is pretty self-explanatory, ever try to guess the password at your friend's house, and it’s literally just their name. Now imagine a large shop doing that, and it becomes a large target for bad actors. Once signed in (on the administrator side), they can change things as they please.

We have one final one for you, session hijacking, you sign in on your account and the attacker takes over and pretends to be you! This is of course far more dangerous if you’re in your bank for example, but nobody wants any of their data taken. If they do this, you may not know what they are doing with your account. There’re a few ways to protect yourself (check out our Top 10 Ways to Keep Yourself Safe), but one big one is policing your cookies. Simply put, a cookie remembers who you are and what the website is allowed to do, so you maybe you want no cookies active (websites ask for permission when you connect) so that the attacker cannot use them against you. You can also always sign out and use 2-factor authentication.

You may be wondering why a network would be unsecure. Like why would a company have these risks, there are a few reasons. Number one is change is hard and expensive so a company may not be motivated to update their system if it has been working so far. They may just be complacent and not believe it is important or they may be uneducated. Some of these issues are business-specific and you can try to pressure your favourites businesses, but the best solution is a VPN. These will protect your data.
